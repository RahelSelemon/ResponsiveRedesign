Redesigning federalreserve.gov

Redesigning a public-facing site to improve responsiveness, accessibility, clarity, and user confidence—regardless of their background.

Why Redesign the Federal Reserve Website?

The federalreserve.gov website plays an important role in public transparency and economic reporting, but its design creates unnecessary barriers for non-expert users. The site is dense, visually overwhelming, and difficult to navigate. Even experienced users may struggle to quickly locate or identify key updates.

The goal was to make economic data easier to navigate, interpret, and trust for the general public. The redesign focuses on simplifying the site’s structure and layout without sacrificing its depth, to better serve a broader audience.

Usability "Audit"

I evaluated the site with a focus on four key areas: efficiency, learnability, memorability, and accessibility. These helped me spot where the experience was falling short in order to make targeted improvements.

Efficiency

  • Dense text with limited spacing makes reading difficult.
  • Too many top-level categories without collapsibility or prioritization.
  • Redundant headlines lead to visual clutter.
  • Chronological updates are useful and should be retained.

Learnability

  • Unclear visual hierarchy between clickable and non-clickable elements.
  • Lack of headers and guiding text makes orientation difficult.
  • Assumes financial literacy without supporting unfamiliar users.
  • Icons are underutilized or misleading.

Memorability

  • Users struggle to retrace their steps due to poor structural logic.
  • Inconsistent section labels and navigation pathways.

Accessibility

  • Small, low-contrast text poses challenges for visually impaired users.
  • Color palette lacks sufficient variation to support readability.

Accessibility Testing & Conceptual Model

I used WAVE to assess technical accessibility, which revealed several violations:

  • Low contrast ratios and small fonts throughout the site.
  • Missing alt text and captions for images and videos.
  • Skipped heading levels and broken ARIA menu attributes.

The site’s structure reflects an archive-style conceptual model—it prioritizes comprehensiveness over intuitive navigation. You can find what you need if you already know where to look, but the experience offers little guidance for unfamiliar users.

It also implies that the intended audience is economically literate (e.g., analysts, policymakers), rather than the general public. My redesign introduces a dashboard-like model aimed at improving scannability and accessibility for a broader audience—without sacrificing depth or credibility.

Reflection

Challenge: Improving the website’s design while ensuring that changes held up across different screen sizes.


How I approached this:

  • Contrast Issues: I created a new color palette with a stronger contrast ratio (8.59) to improve accessibility.
  • Better Spacing: I applied clear padding and margins, using boxes to separate distinct content areas.
  • Text Size and Hierarchy: I increased the minimum text size and added more visual variation to highlight importance and structure.
  • Clutter Reduction: I collapsed navigation items, replaced redundant top nav bars with a footer, and limited the homepage to just the 3 most recent headlines.
  • Graphics: I added more informative icons and subtle animations to signal interactivity.

Conclusions:

These changes make the site feel less overwhelming and easier to navigate. By simplifying layouts—like turning dense charts into quick “at-a-glance” visuals—and adding controls like a “See More” button, users can explore at their own pace. Clearer structure and features like the Sections menu also help new visitors find and remember key content.
